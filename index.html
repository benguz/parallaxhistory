In 1976, I spent some time in a South African jail.
















I mean, I wasn’t charged or convicted with anything. I was just put in cold storage.









I was 22 years old. I was a kid, you know, and I was politically active. I was part of the leadership of the University, and so on.









They turn up at four in the morning at your parents' house and arrest you.









I spent four days in jail. I was alone in the cell, so that was fine. But it's very disturbing to be in a place where you can't tell the difference between night and day at all, you know, for 4 days it's very disorienting.





There were 70 of us in this one cell block. No mixture with ordinary criminals, because they didn't want us to politicize ordinary criminals.









This will not be a nice experience at all. but an important one, because I know what it's like to live in a country where the rule of law doesn't exist.









You have a system in place, if you can imagine, where a white minority controls the upper offices of state, right? So government, police, military, and so on.









You have 80% of the population not part of that system. And there's no room. This is a zero sum game, right? If the whites say, okay, we're gonna open up the system: they are out of power.









This was not a fight that was resolved in the sense that black people then became victorious, and there was victor's justice.









What happened in South Africa was a negotiation. It was a stalemate, a political stalemate. The whites couldn't keep power anymore. They were losing global credibility.









I was never harmed physically. This was a fight. We knew this was a fight, you know? This is not a party.









You get arrested because of it, and it's not nice to be arrested, but I mean I don’t feel like I need to forgive people. What I do feel is people have to explain themselves.









The head of a very very big company is run by a South African… Richemont. And they bought that company on the basis of selling cigarettes in my hometown.









There was recently a puff piece in the FT that the owners fought against Apartheid. Now that is a lie.









The party government introduced the Group Areas act, which is active residential segregation. They forcibly removed the black people living there, and my parents were removed like that.









This company didn’t say a word about it! They have a lot of explaining to do, and they haven’t done it yet.









So that makes me cross, actually.
